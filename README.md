How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating "user stories" help with this?
Interpreting user needs starts with asking who the user is, what they are trying to accomplish, and why that goal matters to them. User stories make this concrete by forcing those three elements into a single, readable statement, "As a [user], I want to [action], so that I can [goal]", that the entire team can reference throughout development. In the SNHU Travel project, writing user stories for Brent, Maria, and Nick helped me move past vague feature descriptions and think about what each traveler actually needed from the application. The acceptance criteria attached to each story then translated those needs into specific, testable conditions, which gave the developer a precise target and gave the tester a foundation for every test case. Without that structure, it is easy to build something technically functional that still fails to serve the user.
How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?
I approach development iteratively, starting with what is known, building something testable, and refining based on feedback rather than trying to plan everything upfront. The Agile practices I found most valuable in this course and plan to carry forward are sprint planning with clearly defined acceptance criteria, daily standups to surface blockers early, and the sprint retrospective as a dedicated space to improve the team's process rather than just the product. I also want to maintain the habit of writing test cases in parallel with development rather than after the fact, which shortened the feedback loop significantly in the SNHU Travel project and caught misalignments before they became expensive to fix.
What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?
Being a good team member means making your work and your blockers visible to the people around you, communicating specifically rather than vaguely, and treating the other roles on the team as partners rather than handoff points. In the SNHU Travel project, the moments where the team functioned best were when the tester proactively flagged ambiguities before development began, and when the developer requested updated artifacts before writing new code rather than proceeding on assumptions. Good team members do not wait for problems to escalate, they raise them early, in the right forum, with enough context for someone else to act on them. That kind of transparency is what Agile is built on, and it is a habit I want to carry into every team I work on.
